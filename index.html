Background & motivation

For MaxCut, depth-\(p\) QAOA prepares \(\lvert\boldsymbol\gamma,\boldsymbol\beta\rangle=\prod_{\ell=1}^{p}e^{-i\beta_\ell B}e^{-i\gamma_\ell C}\lvert +\rangle^{\otimes n}\) and maximizes the expected cut \(\langle C\rangle\) over \(2p\) angles. On hardware, each evaluation of \(\langle C\rangle\) costs many circuit executions and shots, and the variational optimizer must call it repeatedly. The dominant cost is therefore the number of objective queries spent searching for high-quality angles — aggravated by barren plateaus and cost concentration — not the per-circuit depth. The practically relevant question is not "what is the best achievable cut" but "how favorable a cut can a policy reach within a fixed query budget."

The query-cost bottleneck

A high-quality warm start seeds the optimizer inside a high-quality basin, turning a hundreds-of-query angle search into a handful. The question is which warm start, and whether learning earns its keep.

The literature gap

Angle transfer, parameter concentration, classical-relaxation and meta-learning warm starts are well motivated — but rarely tested in controlled, leakage-aware, query-counted head-to-heads against a strong-but-simple spectral baseline.

The risk of over-crediting

Without those controls it is tempting to attribute to a learned model a gain that a one-line spectral rule already delivers. This benchmark is built to detect exactly that.

The precise gap addressed. At depth one the expected cut has an exact closed form (Wang et al., 2018), so the dependence of high-quality angles on local graph structure can be probed analytically and at negligible cost. We ask whether a permutation-invariant, graph-conditioned policy can propose warm-start angles that transfer to unseen graph families and outperform a spectral initializer — and we measure it under matched query budgets with an explicit leakage-free check.

Tutorial and primer for research scientists

QAOA is a hybrid workflow: a quantum circuit evaluates a candidate pair of angles, and a classical optimizer decides what to try next. This page should be read as a query-efficiency and experiment-design contribution. It asks whether learned graph topology provides a useful warm start after controlling for a strong spectral baseline, not whether learning can be credited for gains that a simpler rule already captures.

Novelty highlight

Contributions & novelty

The contribution is methodological, not a solution-quality advantage. Exactly three contributions are new:

  • (1) A proven relabeling-invariant descriptor framework. A fixed-length descriptor \(\phi(G)\) built only from graph invariants — degree statistics, triangle/4-cycle motif densities, a hashed Weisfeiler–Lehman colour histogram, cycle features, Laplacian-spectral moments, and connectivity features — so isomorphic graphs map to identical vectors. Invariance is not merely argued (Theorem 1) but verified numerically to \(10^{-8}\) under random node permutations.
  • (2) A cross-verified, query-counted warm-start benchmark. Every policy proposes initial angles that feed a budgeted coordinate-ascent refiner; every objective query is counted against a fixed budget. The depth-one objective is computed by an analytic per-edge formula (Wang et al.) cross-checked against an exact statevector simulator to \(10^{-9}\), and approximation ratios are exact against a brute-force MaxCut oracle.
  • (3) Family-held-out transfer with leakage controls. Policies are fit on five graph families and evaluated on the sixth held-out family, with a programmatic fingerprint check (family, \(n\), \(\lvert E\rvert\), hashed degree sequence) confirming that test graphs are excluded from training (leakage_clean: true).
Transparent framing (read before you score this). The central finding is a statistical equivalence, reported as such: the learned graph-conditioned policy and the simple spectral initializer reach the same held-out approximation ratio to within statistical resolution. Topology conditioning matches, not exceeds, spectral initialization at depth one — because both encode the same relevant spectral structure. We refrain from superiority and quantum-hardware claims.

Mathematics & method

Five interchangeable warm-start policies feed one query-budgeted refiner; the reward is the exact approximation ratio \(\langle C\rangle/C^\star\) from a brute-force MaxCut oracle.

Cost operator & depth-1 state

$$C=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\tfrac12\big(1-Z_uZ_v\big),\qquad B=\sum_{v}X_v$$

$$\lvert\gamma,\beta\rangle=e^{-i\beta B}\,e^{-i\gamma C}\,\lvert +\rangle^{\otimes n},\qquad \langle C\rangle=\langle\gamma,\beta\lvert\,C\,\rvert\gamma,\beta\rangle$$

The statevector evaluator (cost \(O(p\,n\,2^n)\)) is the unambiguous reference oracle.

Analytic depth-1 per-edge closed form (Wang et al., 2018)

For edge \((u,v)\) with partner-excluded degrees \(d_u,d_v\) and \(f\) common neighbours:

$$\langle C_{uv}\rangle=\tfrac12+\tfrac14\sin(4\beta)\sin\gamma\big(\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma+\cos^{d_v}\!\gamma\big) -\tfrac14\sin^2(2\beta)\,\cos^{\,d_u+d_v-2f}\!\gamma\,\big(1-\cos^{f}2\gamma\big)$$

\(\langle C\rangle=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\langle C_{uv}\rangle\), cost \(O(\lvert E\rvert)\) — the same math the in-browser demo runs.

Descriptor map and its invariance theorem

The learned policy conditions on a fixed-length descriptor concatenating invariant blocks:

$$\phi(G)=\big[\ \underbrace{\text{degree}}_{\text{mean, sd, max, min, density}}\ \big\Vert\ \underbrace{\text{motif}}_{\triangle,\ \text{clustering},\ C_4}\ \big\Vert\ \text{WL}_{\text{hashed}}\ \big\Vert\ \text{cycle}\ \big\Vert\ \underbrace{\text{Laplacian}}_{\lambda_2,\ \text{gap},\ \text{moments}}\ \big\Vert\ \text{connectivity}\ \big]$$

Theorem 1 (relabeling invariance). For any node relabeling \(\pi\) with permutation matrix \(\Pi\), \(\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)\). Sketch: degree/motif/cycle/connectivity features are incidence quantities; the Laplacian transforms as \(\Pi L\Pi^\top\) so its eigenvalues and all spectral moments are fixed; WL refinement permutes node identities but preserves every neighbour-colour multiset, so the histogram is unchanged. Concatenating invariant blocks is invariant.

Experimental design & statistics

The reported scale comes from configs/full.yaml: every number below is generated from fixed seeds, never hand-entered.

Design

  • n = 120 connected graphs — six families (Erdős–Rényi, random regular, Barabási–Albert, Watts–Strogatz, 2D grid, stochastic block), 20 per family.
  • Sizes \(n\in\{10,12,14,16\}\); depth \(p=1\); query budget 40; master seed 0.
  • Full pipeline runs in 84.5 s at 163.3 MB peak on a single laptop CPU.

Statistics & controls

  • Means with 95% CIs \(=1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}\) over the n=120 held-out test evaluations.
  • Family-held-out splits: for each held-out family the learned policy is fit only on the other five.
  • Programmatic leakage check (fingerprint): leakage_clean: true.
  • Closed-form ↔ statevector agreement to \(10^{-9}\); descriptor invariance to \(10^{-8}\).
  • Approx. ratios are exact vs. brute-force \(C^\star\) (enumerating \(2^{n-1}\) bipartitions, tractable for \(n\le16\)).

Structure outperforms random

Every structure-aware policy reaches 0.7846 vs. 0.7327 for random — an absolute gain of 0.052 (≈7% relative), well outside the ±0.0162 random-policy interval.

Learned ≈ spectral (statistical equivalence)

Graph-conditioned and spectral means differ by ~\(4\times10^{-5}\) — three orders of magnitude below their shared ±0.0093 CI. The non-learned topology heuristic also lands at 0.7846. We refrain from any superiority claim.

Query efficiency

Structure-aware policies are near-final on the first query: graph-conditioned 0.7807, topology 0.7775, spectral 0.7388 at one evaluation, vs. random 0.6148. Random's 40-query ratio is exceeded by every structure-aware policy on its first evaluation.

Per-family held-out transfer

Family identity — not the choice among spectral / topology / graph-conditioned — explains essentially all variation; learned and spectral means differ by at most a few parts in \(10^4\) in every family (selected families shown).

Conclusion & impact

At full scale a learned, topology-conditioned warm start reaches the same depth-one approximation ratio as a one-line spectral initializer (0.7846 ± 0.0093 for both, with the non-learned topology heuristic also at 0.7846), while all three clearly improve on random (0.7327). The learned policy does not surpass the spectral baseline — and we resist tuning the benchmark to manufacture an advantage.

What it means

For depth-1 MaxCut on these families, a spectral rule is already on the efficient frontier: both it and the learned descriptor encode the same Laplacian-spectral signal, so once even a modest refinement budget is allowed, any early advantage is equalized.

Where the value is

A reusable, transparent yardstick — proven-invariant descriptors, query-counted refiner, two cross-verified evaluators, exact ratios, family-held-out splits with leakage checks — plus a clear query-efficiency finding: structure-aware policies are near-final on the first query.

Outlook

The open question this sharpens: can any learned policy extend its early-budget edge through refinement rather than seeing it erased? A trained GNN on a larger, more diverse corpus, or deeper circuits, may separate from the spectral prior — the framework is built to drop one in.

Stated limitations (from the paper). Depth one only (where the exact closed form renders the landscape inexpensive to evaluate and smooth); \(n\le16\) so exact MaxCut is tractable for ground-truth ratios; synthetic graphs (motivated by, not drawn from, real data); a single master seed, so CIs are over the 120 graphs within one run rather than independent dataset redraws; noiseless classical evaluations without shot or device noise; and none of the policies reached the 0.95 target within 40 queries. Quantum-hardware and superiority claims are avoided.
