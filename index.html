The problem

QAOA prepares a variational state \(|\gamma,\beta\rangle\) and maximizes the expected MaxCut value \(\langle C\rangle\), where \(C=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\tfrac{1}{2}(1-Z_uZ_v)\). The bottleneck is not the circuit — it is the number of objective evaluations spent hunting for good angles, since each evaluation costs many shots. A good warm start turns that search from hundreds of queries into a few.

Why topology

Good angles are not random — they are a smooth function of graph structure (degree, triangles, spectrum). We learn that function once and reuse it.

Why invariance

A graph has no canonical node order. Our descriptors are graph invariants, so the policy gives the same answer under any relabeling — verified in the test suite.

Why biology

Molecular-interaction and patient-similarity graphs are exactly these families: scale-free hubs, small-world, community structure.

Method

Five interchangeable warm-start policies feed one query-budgeted refiner; the reward is the exact approximation ratio from a brute-force MaxCut oracle.

1 · Descriptors
degree · motif · Weisfeiler–Lehman · cycle · Laplacian · connectivity — all order-invariant
2 · Policy
kNN over descriptors → warm-start \((\gamma,\beta)\); optional torch GNN backend
3 · Refiner
coordinate-ascent under a fixed query budget; every evaluation is counted
4 · Audited reward
exact MaxCut oracle → approximation ratio; no fabricated numbers

Why the demo is exact

For depth-1 QAOA on MaxCut, the per-edge expectation has a closed form (Wang et al., 2018). With \(d_u,d_v\) the partner-excluded degrees and \(f\) the common neighbours of an edge \((u,v)\):

$$\langle C_{uv}\rangle=\tfrac12+\tfrac14\sin(4\beta)\sin\gamma\big(\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma+\cos^{d_v}\!\gamma\big) -\tfrac14\sin^2(2\beta)\,\cos^{\,d_u+d_v-2f}\!\gamma\,\big(1-\cos^{f}2\gamma\big)$$

The package ships both this O(|E|) form and a full statevector simulator, and a test asserts they agree to \(10^{-9}\) on random graphs from every family — so the fast in-browser math is the trusted math.
