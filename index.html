Background and Motivation

On near-term hardware, the dominant cost of the quantum approximate optimization algorithm is not the depth of the circuit but the number of objective evaluations consumed while searching for good variational angles.

The quantum approximate optimization algorithm (QAOA) is a leading candidate for near-term quantum advantage on combinatorial problems and a workhorse benchmark for variational algorithms in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era. For the canonical MaxCut problem — NP-hard, with the celebrated Goemans–Williamson classical guarantee — a depth-$p$ circuit alternates a cost-Hamiltonian phase and a transverse-field mixer parameterized by $2p$ angles $(\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta})$, and maximizes the expected cut $\langle C(\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta})\rangle$. On real devices each evaluation of $\langle C\rangle$ costs many circuit executions and shots, and the variational optimizer must call the objective repeatedly. Angle optimization is therefore a primary bottleneck, aggravated by barren plateaus and cost concentration in high-dimensional landscapes. The practically relevant quantity is not only the best achievable cut, but the cut a policy can reach within a fixed query budget.

A natural remedy is a warm start: supply good initial angles so the optimizer begins inside a high-quality basin. Several observations motivate this — the QAOA objective concentrates across instances of a given structure, optimal parameters transfer between random graphs and across sizes, and dedicated initialization schemes (annealing schedules, relaxation rounding, classical surrogates, meta-learning) can place the optimizer well. At depth one in particular, the expected cut admits an exact closed form, so the dependence of good angles on local graph structure can be probed analytically and at negligible cost.

The open problem. This invites a learning question: can a policy that conditions on graph topology — ideally a permutation-invariant representation in the spirit of graph neural networks and Weisfeiler–Lehman kernels — propose warm-start angles that transfer to unseen graph families and outperform a simpler initializer? The literature is rich in proposals but comparatively sparse in controlled, leakage-aware, query-counted head-to-head evaluations against a strong-but-simple spectral baseline. Absent such controls it is easy to over-credit a learned component for gains that a one-line spectral rule already delivers.

Mathematical Preliminaries

The formal objects a reader must command: the MaxCut cost operator and mixer, the depth-one state, the per-edge closed-form estimator, and the relabeling-invariant descriptor generator.

Graph and spectral objects

$G=(V,E)$ is a finite simple undirected graph on $n=|V|$ vertices, adjacency $A\in\{0,1\}^{n\times n}$ (symmetric, zero diagonal), degree matrix $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_n)$ with $d_v=\sum_u A_{uv}$, and combinatorial Laplacian $L=D-A$.

A relabeling is a permutation $\pi\in S_n$ with permutation matrix $\Pi$; the relabeled graph has adjacency $A(\pi G)=\Pi A\Pi^\top$. We write $\lambda_2$ for the algebraic connectivity (second-smallest eigenvalue of $L$).

Cost Hamiltonian, mixer, and state

For MaxCut the cost operator is $C=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\tfrac12(1-Z_uZ_v)$ and the mixer is $B=\sum_v X_v$. The depth-one state is

$|\gamma,\beta\rangle=e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}\,e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}\,|+\rangle^{\otimes n}.$

The objective is the expected cut $\langle C(\gamma,\beta)\rangle=\langle\gamma,\beta|\,C\,|\gamma,\beta\rangle$, maximized over $(\gamma,\beta)$ at $p=1$.

Depth-one per-edge estimator

For an edge $(u,v)\in E$, let $d_u,d_v$ be the numbers of neighbours of $u$ and $v$ other than the partner, and $f$ the number of common neighbours. The expected per-edge contribution has the closed form (Wang et al.):

$$\langle C_{uv}\rangle=\tfrac12+\tfrac14\sin(4\beta)\sin\gamma\,\bigl[\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma+\cos^{d_v}\!\gamma\bigr]-\tfrac14\sin^2(2\beta)\,\cos^{\,d_u+d_v-2f}\!\gamma\,\bigl[1-\cos^{f}(2\gamma)\bigr],$$

and the total expected cut is $\langle C\rangle=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\langle C_{uv}\rangle$ by linearity. This $O(|E|)$ analytic estimator is the same quantity as the exact $O(p\,n\,2^n)$ statevector expectation (cross-verified to $10^{-9}$), and the quality of a cut is the approximation ratio $\langle C\rangle/C^\star$ against the true MaxCut $C^\star$ obtained by exact enumeration of the $2^{n-1}$ distinct bipartitions.

Relabeling-invariant descriptor generator

The graph-conditioned policy reads a fixed-length descriptor $\phi(G)\in\mathbb{R}^m$ formed by concatenating six blocks of graph invariants: degree statistics (mean, std, max, min, density); motif densities (triangle density, average clustering, a 4-cycle density from $\operatorname{tr}(A^4)$); a hashed two-round, eight-bin Weisfeiler–Lehman colour histogram; cycle features (fraction of nodes in a triangle, inverse-girth proxy); Laplacian features ($\lambda_2$, spectral gap, mean and variance of the normalized-Laplacian spectrum); and connectivity features (degree assortativity, global clustering). Every component is a multiset, spectral, or trace quantity, so $\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)$ for all $\pi\in S_n$ (proven below, verified to $10^{-8}$).

Problem Formulation

A precise, query-counted statement of the warm-start transfer question, evaluated under matched budgets with leakage control.

Fix depth $p=1$ and a per-graph query budget $B$. A warm-start policy maps a graph to initial angles $(\gamma_0,\beta_0)$ that seed a coordinate-ascent refiner; the refiner counts every call to the objective $\langle C\rangle$ and halts when the budget is exhausted. The running-best approximation ratio as a function of the number of evaluations — the query-budget frontier — is the hardware-relevant comparison.

Given a corpus of connected graphs partitioned into families, and a partition into family-held-out train/test folds with a programmatic no-leakage guarantee, the problem is to determine whether a learned, topology-conditioned policy attains a strictly higher held-out approximation ratio (at matched budget $B$) than a one-line spectral initializer, the non-learned topology heuristic, and uninformed random initialization. The five policies compared are:

  • Random — $\gamma_0\sim\mathcal{U}(0,\pi)$, $\beta_0\sim\mathcal{U}(0,\pi/2)$.
  • Spectral — $\gamma_0=\operatorname{clip}\!\bigl(\pi/[2(1+\lambda_2)],0,\pi\bigr)$ from the algebraic connectivity, $\beta_0=\pi/8$.
  • Topology — $\gamma_0=\arctan(1/\sqrt{\max(1,\bar d)})$ from the mean degree $\bar d$, $\beta_0=\pi/8$ (non-learned).
  • Graph-conditioned — the learned policy: a $k$-NN regressor ($k{=}3$) over standardized relabeling-invariant descriptors, fit on training families only.
  • RL (CEM) — a cross-entropy-method refiner (population 8, elite 3, 4 iterations) seeded by the graph-conditioned proposal.

Principal Contributions

The contribution is methodological — a verified framework and an honest, controlled finding — not a superiority claim.

  1. A relabeling-invariant descriptor framework.A fixed-length graph descriptor built only from graph invariants (degree, motif, hashed Weisfeiler–Lehman, cycle, Laplacian-spectral, and connectivity blocks), with invariance to node relabeling proven and verified numerically to $10^{-8}$ under random permutations.
  2. A cross-verified, query-counted warm-start benchmark.Every policy proposes initial angles feeding a budgeted coordinate-ascent refiner; every objective query is counted against a fixed budget, and the depth-one objective is evaluated by an analytic per-edge formula cross-checked against an exact statevector simulator to $10^{-9}$. Approximation ratios are exact, against a brute-force MaxCut oracle.
  3. Family-held-out transfer with leakage controls.Policies are fit on a subset of graph families and evaluated on a held-out family, with a fingerprint-based programmatic check confirming that no test graph leaks into training (leakage_clean: true).
  4. An honest, controlled finding plus a query-efficiency benefit.Topology-conditioned warm starts match rather than exceed spectral initialization (both 0.7846 ± 0.0093), while every structure-aware policy reaches near-final quality on the very first objective evaluation — a conservatively stated efficiency benefit.

Methodological Novelty

The novelty lies in the controls, not in a new model: a leakage-aware, query-counted, cross-verified head-to-head against a strong-but-simple spectral baseline, with provable invariance.

Relative to the warm-start / angle-transfer literature

Brandão et al. (concentration), Galda et al. and Shaydulin et al. (parameter transfer), Sack–Serbyn (annealing schedules), Egger et al. (warm-start rounding), and meta-learning approaches (Verdon et al., Khairy et al.) propose initializers but rarely test them under matched query budgets with leakage-controlled, family-held-out transfer against a spectral rule. This benchmark supplies exactly that control and finds the learned gain over spectral is statistically null on final ratio.

Relative to graph learning for combinatorial optimization

Work building on Weisfeiler–Lehman kernels and message-passing GNNs (Xu et al., Gilmer et al., Veličković et al.; Dai et al., Cappart et al.) motivates graph-conditioned policies. Here the descriptor is a proven relabeling-invariant, dependency-light surrogate for a message-passing GNN — with WL refinement shown to upper-bound message-passing expressivity — into which a trained GNN backend can be substituted without changing the harness.

Relative to depth-one QAOA analysis

The Wang et al. per-edge closed form is used not merely to evaluate angles cheaply, but as one of two independent evaluators: it is formally derived and then certified equal to the matrix-free statevector simulator (to $10^{-9}$), so the fast $O(|E|)$ objective is provably the exact circuit expectation rather than an approximation.

Reporting discipline

Every reported number is generated by the accompanying code from fixed seeds and written to a single results/summary.json; an audit script checks that tables and figures trace to artifacts and that no unsupported superiority phrasing appears. The null result is reported plainly, with the $0.95$-target miss and synthetic-graph scope left visible.

Theoretical Guarantees

Each result is transcribed from the paper's Theory section and underwrites a specific experimental claim.

Lemma 1 · ℤ₂ reduction and oracle correctness

$C(-z)=C(z)$ for all $z\in\{+1,-1\}^n$; fixing $z_0=+1$ and enumerating the $2^{n-1}$ remaining configurations visits every cut exactly once up to global flip, and the maximum equals the true MaxCut $C^\star$.

Certifies that every approximation ratio $\langle C\rangle/C^\star$ is measured against ground truth, not a relaxation bound.
Lemma 2 · Factorization of the mixer unitary

$e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}=\bigotimes_{v=1}^{n}\bigl(\cos\beta\,\mathbb{I}-\mathrm{i}\sin\beta\,X\bigr)$, so the mixer acts by $n$ independent $2\times2$ operations and is never assembled as a $2^n\times2^n$ matrix.

Makes the reference statevector evaluator cost $O(p\,n\,2^n)$ rather than $O(2^{2n})$.
Proposition 3 · Depth-one per-edge closed form

$\langle C_{uv}\rangle=\tfrac12+\tfrac14\sin(4\beta)\sin\gamma\,[\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma+\cos^{d_v}\!\gamma]-\tfrac14\sin^2(2\beta)\cos^{\,d_u+d_v-2f}\!\gamma\,[1-\cos^{f}(2\gamma)]$, with $\langle C\rangle=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\langle C_{uv}\rangle$.

The $O(|E|)$ objective used everywhere; a depth-one specialization of the light-cone argument.
Corollary 4 · Closed-form / statevector equivalence

For every $(\gamma,\beta)$ and every $G$, the per-edge closed form summed over $E$ equals the statevector expectation $\langle\gamma,\beta|C|\gamma,\beta\rangle$; the two are the same real-analytic function and any discrepancy is floating-point only (verified to $10^{-9}$).

Licenses using the fast closed form everywhere in place of the simulator.
Theorem 6 · Relabeling invariance of the descriptor map

For every permutation $\pi\in S_n$, $\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)$ componentwise. In particular the learned regressor receives identical inputs for isomorphic graphs and cannot exploit node-order leakage.

Makes family-held-out transfer well posed; verified numerically to $10^{-8}$.
Proposition 7 · WL equivariance and message-passing upper bound

The Weisfeiler–Lehman colour multiset after any fixed number of rounds is permutation invariant, and any message-passing GNN with permutation-invariant readout on the same initial features cannot distinguish two graphs that WL fails to distinguish.

Justifies the WL histogram as a principled, dependency-light stand-in for a GNN.
Corollary 8 · The learned policy is a deterministic class function

The graph-conditioned policy $\Theta(G)=\tfrac1k\sum_{j\in N_k(G)}\theta_j$ satisfies $\Theta(\pi G)=\Theta(G)$ and is a deterministic function of $G$ given the seeded training set.

The warm start is a principled estimator on isomorphism classes, reproducible across runs.
Proposition 9 · Monotonicity and warm-start floor of the refiner

The running-best ratio $r_t=\max(r_{t-1},\rho_t)$ is monotone non-decreasing in $t$ and satisfies $r_t\ge r_0$ for all $t$; a policy with high $r_0$ cannot be overtaken below $r_0$.

Explains the frontier's shape — structure-aware head starts are equalized onto a common ratio near 0.7846 rather than any collapsing.

Significance and Impact

A reusable, honest yardstick for warm-start research, a sharpened open question, and an actionable conclusion for practitioners — with the study's limitations kept in plain view.

For the field. The framework — a proven relabeling-invariant descriptor, a query-counted refiner, two cross-verified objective evaluators, exact approximation ratios, and family-held-out splits with leakage checks — forms a reusable benchmark against which future, and genuinely stronger, warm-start policies can be measured. The controlled null result is itself informative: a structured generalization of the angle-transfer literature, it confirms that good depth-one angles are largely a function of coarse graph structure, which both the spectral rule and the learned descriptor capture.

For downstream application. The efficiency framing is the practically load-bearing one: structure-aware policies reach near-final quality from the very first query — the property that matters when each query is an expensive circuit execution. The benchmark sharpens a concrete open question — whether any learned policy can extend that early-budget edge through the refinement phase rather than seeing it erased — and provides the harness to test it, including a drop-in slot for a trained message-passing GNN backend.

Honest limitations (carried from the paper, unmodified).
  • The study is at depth one, where an exact closed form makes the objective cheap and smooth; deeper circuits have rougher landscapes where a learned policy may have more room, and the closed form no longer applies.
  • Graphs are small ($n\le16$) so exact MaxCut ground truth is tractable; behaviour at advantage-relevant sizes is untested.
  • Graphs are synthetic; the reported run uses a single master seed, so confidence intervals are over the 120 graphs within that run, not over independent dataset redraws.
  • Objective values are noiseless classical evaluations with no shot or device noise; no quantum-hardware or superiority claim is made. The learned backend is a dependency-light descriptor regressor standing in for a GNN.

Reproducibility

CPU-only, no quantum-software dependency. Every number is regenerated from fixed seeds and written to a single source of truth, results/summary.json.

The complete reference implementation — graph generators, the relabeling-invariant descriptors, the analytic and statevector QAOA evaluators, the budgeted refiner, all five warm-start policies, family-held-out splitting and leakage checks, metrics, the experiment runner, the figure and table generators, and the test suite (including the closed-form-versus-statevector and descriptor-invariance checks) — accompanies the manuscript in submission/code. It depends only on NumPy, SciPy, NetworkX, scikit-learn, Matplotlib, and PyYAML. The pip-installable package is topoqaoa (v0.1.0, MIT).
