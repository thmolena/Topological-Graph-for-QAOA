\begin{abstract}
\noindent
On near-term quantum hardware, the dominant cost of the quantum approximate optimization algorithm (QAOA) is the number of circuit evaluations spent searching for good variational angles, and learned, topology-aware warm starts are an appealing way to cut that cost---yet whether such a warm start truly outperforms a simple spectral initializer is rarely tested under matched query budgets with leakage-controlled transfer. We introduce a fully reproducible benchmark that pairs a fixed-length, \emph{relabeling-invariant} graph descriptor (degree, motif, hashed Weisfeiler--Lehman, cycle, Laplacian-spectral and connectivity features, verified invariant to node relabeling to $10^{-8}$) with a query-counted depth-one MaxCut refiner whose objective is cross-verified by two independent evaluators (an analytic per-edge closed form and an exact statevector simulator that agree to $10^{-9}$). We evaluate five warm-start policies---random, spectral, a non-learned topology heuristic, a learned graph-conditioned descriptor regressor, and a cross-entropy refiner---on $\sync{120}$ connected graphs from six families, using family-held-out splits with an explicit no-leakage check. The learned graph-conditioned policy reaches a held-out approximation ratio of $\sync{0.7846}\pm\sync{0.0093}$, statistically indistinguishable from the spectral baseline ($\sync{0.7846}\pm\sync{0.0093}$) and the topology heuristic ($\sync{0.7846}\pm\sync{0.0093}$), while clearly improving on random initialization ($\sync{0.7327}\pm\sync{0.0162}$). We report this plainly: topology-conditioned warm starts \emph{match} rather than exceed spectral initialization, because both capture the same relevant spectral structure of these MaxCut landscapes. The contribution is therefore methodological---a proven-invariant descriptor framework, a cross-verified and query-counted warm-start benchmark, and leakage-controlled family-held-out transfer---together with a query-efficiency finding: structure-aware policies reach near-final quality on the first evaluation. The study is intentionally modest in scale, uses synthetic graphs, and makes no superiority or hardware claims; every number is generated by the accompanying code from fixed seeds.
\end{abstract}

The quantum approximate optimization algorithm (QAOA)~\cite{farhi2014quantum,hadfield2019quantum} is a leading candidate for near-term quantum advantage on combinatorial problems and a workhorse benchmark for variational quantum algorithms in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era~\cite{preskill2018quantum,cerezo2021variational}. For the canonical MaxCut problem---a textbook NP-hard task~\cite{karp1972reducibility} with a celebrated classical approximation guarantee~\cite{goemans1995improved}---a depth-$p$ QAOA circuit alternates a cost-Hamiltonian phase and a transverse-field mixer---itself a product-formula (Trotterized Hamiltonian-simulation) circuit whose gate cost and approximation error are governed by the same operator-exponential structure analyzed for Hamiltonian simulation~\cite{jing2026complexity,nguyen2025zassenhaus,liergtc}---parameterized by $2p$ angles $(\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta})$, and maximizes the expected cut $\expval{C(\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta})}$ over those angles. On real devices, every evaluation of $\expval{C}$ costs many circuit executions and shots, and the variational optimizer must call the objective repeatedly; angle optimization is widely recognized as a primary bottleneck, exacerbated by barren plateaus and cost concentration in high-dimensional landscapes~\cite{mcclean2018barren,brandao2018fixed,zhou2020quantum}. The practically relevant question is therefore not only ``what is the best achievable cut'' but ``how good a cut can a policy reach within a \emph{fixed query budget}''.

This query-counted framing follows the resource-accounting perspective of
graph-conditioned trust-region QAOA, where learned uncertainty is used to
allocate a small number of objective evaluations rather than merely predict a
single angle vector~\cite{uqqaoa}.  The depth-one benchmark here is
deliberately narrower: it asks whether relabeling-invariant graph descriptors
add value beyond a spectral initializer before moving to higher-depth trust
regions.  Because QAOA and annealing-style warm starts are product-formula
circuits, we also situate the benchmark alongside recent fixed-depth and
representation-theoretic Hamiltonian-simulation work by Nguyen and
Jing~\cite{nguyen2025zassenhaus,jing2026complexity}; those citations motivate
the simulation context, not a claim that this paper modifies the Hamiltonian
simulation error bounds.

A natural way to reduce this cost is a \emph{warm start}: supply good initial angles so the optimizer begins inside a high-quality basin. Several lines of work motivate this. The QAOA objective concentrates across typical instances of a given structure~\cite{brandao2018fixed}, optimal parameters transfer between random graphs and across problem sizes~\cite{galda2021transferability,shaydulin2023parameter}, and dedicated initialization schemes---continuation/annealing schedules~\cite{sack2021quantum}, classical-relaxation rounding~\cite{egger2021warmstarting}, and classical-surrogate or meta-learning approaches~\cite{streif2020training,verdon2019learning,khairy2020learning}---can place the optimizer in a good basin. At depth one in particular, the expected cut admits an exact closed form~\cite{wang2018quantum}, so the dependence of good angles on local graph structure can be probed analytically and at negligible cost.

This invites an obvious learning question: can a policy that \emph{conditions on graph topology}---ideally a permutation-invariant graph representation in the spirit of graph neural networks and Weisfeiler--Lehman kernels~\cite{shervashidze2011weisfeiler,weisfeiler1968reduction,kipf2017semi,gilmer2017neural,xu2019powerful,velivckovic2018graph}, which have been productive for combinatorial optimization~\cite{dai2017learning,cappart2023combinatorial}---propose warm-start angles that transfer to \emph{unseen} graph families and beat simpler initializers? The literature is rich in proposals but comparatively sparse in controlled, leakage-aware, query-counted head-to-head evaluations against a strong-but-simple spectral baseline. Without such controls it is easy to over-credit a learned component for gains a one-line spectral rule already delivers.

We address this gap with a deliberately honest, fully reproducible benchmark. Our contributions are threefold. \textbf{(1)~A relabeling-invariant descriptor framework.} We define a fixed-length graph descriptor built only from graph invariants---degree statistics, triangle/4-cycle motif densities, a hashed Weisfeiler--Lehman color histogram, cycle features, Laplacian-spectral moments, and connectivity features---so that isomorphic graphs map to identical descriptors. Invariance is not merely argued but verified to $10^{-8}$ under random node permutations. \textbf{(2)~A cross-verified, query-counted warm-start benchmark.} Every policy proposes initial angles that feed a budgeted coordinate-ascent refiner; every objective query is counted against a fixed budget, and the depth-one objective is evaluated by an analytic per-edge formula~\cite{wang2018quantum} cross-checked against an exact statevector simulator to $10^{-9}$. Approximation ratios are exact, computed against a brute-force MaxCut oracle. \textbf{(3)~Family-held-out transfer with leakage controls.} Policies are fit on a subset of graph families and evaluated on a held-out family, with a programmatic check that no test graph leaks into training.

Our central finding is reported without embellishment. The learned graph-conditioned policy and the simple spectral initializer reach \emph{the same} held-out approximation ratio to within statistical resolution, both clearly above random initialization. Topology-conditioned warm starts thus \emph{match}, not exceed, spectral initialization for depth-one QAOA. We argue this is the expected outcome---both encode the relevant spectral structure of these landscapes---and we present it as a useful, controlled result together with a reusable evaluation framework and a clear query-efficiency benefit, rather than as a claim of superiority on solution quality. The study makes no quantum-hardware claim, addresses only depth one and exact-MaxCut-tractable sizes, and uses synthetic graphs.

\subsection{Problem setup and the budgeted-warm-start framework}
For MaxCut on $G=(V,E)$ the cost operator is $C=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\tfrac12(1-Z_uZ_v)$, and depth-$p$ QAOA prepares $\ket{\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta}}=\prod_{\ell=1}^{p}e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta_\ell B}e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma_\ell C}\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$ with mixer $B=\sum_v X_v$, and maximizes $\expval{C(\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta})}$. We work at depth $p=1$ and compute $\expval{C}$ two independent ways: a full statevector simulator (cost $O(p\,n\,2^n)$, the reference oracle) and the analytic per-edge closed form of Wang et al.~\cite{wang2018quantum} (cost $O(\abs{E})$; Methods, Eq.~\eqref{eq:closedform}). The two agree to $10^{-9}$ on graphs from every family, so all reported objective values are exact depth-one expectations. The quality of a cut is the approximation ratio $\expval{C}/C^\star$ against the true MaxCut $C^\star$, obtained by exact enumeration of the $2^{n-1}$ distinct bipartitions (tractable for $n\le16$); all ratios in this paper are ground truth, not relaxation bounds.

Figure~\ref{fig:schematic} summarizes the end-to-end pipeline. A warm-start policy maps a graph to initial angles $(\gamma_0,\beta_0)$ that seed a coordinate-ascent refiner. The refiner \emph{counts every call} to the objective and stops when a per-graph budget of evaluations is exhausted, so the running-best approximation ratio as a function of the number of evaluations---the \emph{query-budget frontier}---is a faithful, hardware-relevant comparison. We compare five policies: \emph{random} (uniform angles), \emph{spectral} ($\gamma_0$ from Laplacian algebraic connectivity, $\beta_0=\pi/8$), \emph{topology} (a non-learned fixed-angle heuristic scaled by mean degree), \emph{graph-conditioned} (the learned policy: a $k$-nearest-neighbour regressor over standardized relabeling-invariant descriptors, fit on training graphs only), and \emph{rl} (a cross-entropy-method refiner seeded by the graph-conditioned proposal). To test transfer honestly, for each held-out family we fit the learned policy only on graphs from the other five families and evaluate on the held-out one; a fingerprint-based leakage check confirms no test graph appears in training.

\subsection{Reported scale and reproducibility}
All numbers below come from the reported-scale configuration \texttt{configs/full.yaml}: six families---Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi~\cite{erdos1959random}, random regular, Barab\'asi--Albert (scale-free)~\cite{barabasi1999emergence}, Watts--Strogatz (small-world)~\cite{watts1998collective}, two-dimensional grid, and stochastic block~\cite{holland1983stochastic}---with $\sync{20}$ graphs per family ($\sync{120}$ graphs total), sizes $n\in\{10,12,14,16\}$, depth-1, query budget $\sync{40}$, and master seed $0$. The leakage check confirmed the run is clean (\texttt{leakage\_clean}: true). The whole pipeline runs in \SI{84.5}{\second} with a peak memory of \SI{163.3}{\mega\byte} on a single laptop CPU. Means are reported with $95\%$ confidence intervals ($1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}$) over the $\sync{120}$ test evaluations. Every value is written directly to \texttt{results/summary.json} by the runner and propagated to the tables and figures by the plotting scripts; none are entered by hand.

\subsection{Approximation ratio at matched budget}
Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the held-out approximation ratio (mean over the $n=\sync{120}$ test evaluations, $95\%$ confidence intervals), the mean queries-to-target (target $0.95$), and the target hit rate for all five policies. Three observations stand out. First, every structure-aware policy improves substantially on random initialization: the spectral, topology, and graph-conditioned policies all reach $\sync{0.7846}$, versus $\sync{0.7327}$ for random---an absolute gain of $\sync{0.052}$ ($\sync{\approx 7\%}$ relative). Second, the learned graph-conditioned policy is statistically indistinguishable from the simple spectral baseline: their means differ by $\sync{4\times10^{-5}}$, two orders of magnitude smaller than the $\pm\sync{0.0093}$ confidence interval they share, and the non-learned topology heuristic also lands at $\sync{0.7846}$. Third, the cross-entropy refiner (\textsc{rl}) is marginally below the others at $\sync{0.7808}\pm\sync{0.0092}$, consistent with its stochastic sampling spending part of the budget exploring. No policy reached the stringent $0.95$ target within $\sync{40}$ queries, so the queries-to-target equals the budget ($\sync{40.00}$) and the hit rate is $\sync{0.00}$ for all policies; we report this transparently rather than relaxing the target.

\subsection{Query-budget frontier}
Figure~\ref{fig:frontier} plots the mean running-best approximation ratio as a function of the query budget (1--40 evaluations) per policy---the central efficiency view of the benchmark. The structure-aware policies start high: at the very first query the graph-conditioned policy already attains $\sync{0.7807}$, the topology heuristic $\sync{0.7775}$, and the spectral policy $\sync{0.7388}$, whereas random starts at $\sync{0.6148}$. As the budget grows, all curves rise and the three structure-aware policies converge tightly onto one another near $\sync{0.7846}$; random climbs steadily but never closes the gap, reaching only $\sync{0.7327}$ at $\sync{40}$ queries. Thus random's \emph{final} ($\sync{40}$-query) ratio is already exceeded by every structure-aware policy on its \emph{first} evaluation, and random never reaches the graph-conditioned policy's one-query ratio anywhere within the budget---a conservatively stated query-efficiency benefit. The graph-conditioned policy holds a slim lead over spectral at small budgets but this advantage is absorbed by the refiner well before the budget is exhausted, so the final ratios coincide. The \textsc{rl} curve rises from a lower start ($\sync{0.6835}$ at one query, because its first samples explore) and plateaus just under the others.

\subsection{Final approximation ratio is within noise across structural policies}
We state the limiting result as plainly as the supporting one. On \emph{final} approximation ratio at the full budget (Table~\ref{tab:main}), the graph-conditioned ($\sync{0.7846}$), topology ($\sync{0.7846}$), and spectral ($\sync{0.7846}$) policies are statistically indistinguishable: their means differ by $\sim\!\sync{10^{-4}}$, roughly two orders of magnitude smaller than the $\pm\sync{0.0093}$ confidence interval. We therefore make no claim that the learned graph-conditioned policy beats the spectral or fixed-topology heuristics on approximation ratio at the tested scale; on that metric they are equivalent. The only ratio difference that exceeds the confidence interval is over uninformed initialization: every structure-aware policy improves on random by $\sync{0.052}$ ($\sync{\approx 7\%}$ relative, from $\sync{0.7327}$ to $\sync{0.7846}$), well outside the $\pm\sync{0.0162}$ random-policy interval. In short, structure helps, but the specific form of structure-awareness does not change the final ratio; what conditioning on the full topology descriptor buys is a near-optimal first guess that transfers.

\subsection{Per-family transfer}
Figure~\ref{fig:family} breaks the held-out approximation ratio down by graph family. The structure-aware policies track each other closely within every family, and the family identity---not the choice among spectral, topology, or graph-conditioned---explains essentially all of the variation. Cuts are easiest on community-structured stochastic-block graphs (spectral $\sync{0.8286}$, graph-conditioned $\sync{0.8288}$) and Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi graphs (spectral $\sync{0.8269}$, graph-conditioned $\sync{0.8269}$), and hardest on grids (spectral $\sync{0.6948}$, graph-conditioned $\sync{0.6948}$), where the locally lattice-like structure makes depth-one MaxCut intrinsically harder; random initialization is markedly worse on grids ($\sync{0.5979}$). On random-regular graphs all structure-aware policies and even random initialization nearly coincide ($\sync{\approx 0.772}$), as expected for degree-homogeneous instances where there is little spectral structure to exploit. Crucially, the graph-conditioned and spectral means differ by at most a few parts in $10^{4}$ in \emph{every} family---far inside the per-family confidence intervals---so we find no family in which learned conditioning produces a statistically meaningful advantage over the spectral prior. The relabeling-invariant descriptor is nonetheless what makes this transfer well-posed: a held-out scale-free or lattice graph is mapped to the same fixed-length invariant feature vector as the training graphs, so the nearest-neighbour angle lookup is defined even for a family never seen during fitting.

\subsection{Cross-verification and invariance}
Two internal correctness checks underpin the benchmark. The depth-one objective is computed by the analytic per-edge closed form~\cite{wang2018quantum} and independently by an exact statevector simulator; across all six families and a panel of angle settings these two evaluators agree to better than $10^{-9}$, so the fast closed form used throughout is provably the same objective as the unambiguous simulator. Separately, the topology descriptor is verified to be invariant to node relabeling: under repeated random permutations the descriptor vector is unchanged to $10^{-8}$. These guarantees ensure that the reported ratios are exact and that the learned policy genuinely sees only order-invariant graph information. The next section places each of these guarantees on a rigorous footing.

\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}
This section formalizes, and proves in full, the structural guarantees on which the benchmark rests: that the exact oracle is correct and the global $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry may be quotiented (Lemma~\ref{lem:z2}); that the transverse-field mixer factorizes so the simulator is matrix-free (Lemma~\ref{lem:matrixfree}); that the depth-one expectation admits the per-edge closed form used everywhere, and that this closed form equals the statevector expectation exactly (Proposition~\ref{prop:closedform} and Corollary~\ref{cor:crossverify}); that traces of adjacency powers are relabeling invariant (Lemma~\ref{lem:trace}); that the full descriptor map is relabeling invariant (Theorem~\ref{thm:qaoa-invariance}); that Weisfeiler--Lehman refinement is permutation equivariant and upper-bounds message-passing expressivity (Proposition~\ref{prop:wl}); that the learned $k$-nearest-neighbour policy is therefore well defined on isomorphism classes and deterministic (Corollary~\ref{cor:knn}); and that the refiner's running-best curve is monotone and never falls below its warm start, which is exactly why an early head start is absorbed (Proposition~\ref{prop:monotone}). Each result names the experiment it underwrites.

\subsection{Notation and standing assumptions}
Throughout, $G=(V,E)$ is a finite simple undirected graph on $n=\abs{V}$ vertices with adjacency matrix $A\in\{0,1\}^{n\times n}$ (symmetric, zero diagonal), degree matrix $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_n)$ with $d_v=\sum_u A_{uv}$, and combinatorial Laplacian $L=D-A$. A node relabeling is a permutation $\pi\in S_n$ with permutation matrix $\Pi$ (entries $\Pi_{\pi(i),i}=1$, else $0$); the relabeled graph $\pi G$ has adjacency $A(\pi G)=\Pi A\Pi^\top$. We write $\ket{z}$ for the computational basis state indexed by a spin configuration $z\in\{+1,-1\}^n$, $Z_v\ket{z}=z_v\ket{z}$, and $X_v$ for the bit flip on qubit $v$. The cost operator is $C=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\tfrac12(1-Z_uZ_v)$, the mixer is $B=\sum_v X_v$, and the depth-one state is $\ket{\gamma,\beta}=e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$. We assume (as in the reported configuration) that the exact oracle is only invoked for $n\le 20$, so brute-force enumeration is well defined.

\subsection{Correctness of the exact MaxCut oracle}
\begin{lemma}[$\mathbb{Z}_2$ reduction and oracle correctness]
\label{lem:z2}
The cut value $C(z)=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\tfrac12(1-z_uz_v)$ satisfies $C(-z)=C(z)$ for all $z\in\{+1,-1\}^n$. Consequently, fixing $z_0=+1$ and enumerating the $2^{n-1}$ configurations of $(z_1,\dots,z_{n-1})$ visits every cut exactly once up to global flip, and the maximum so obtained equals the true MaxCut value $C^\star=\max_{z}C(z)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
For any $z$, $(-z)_u(-z)_v=z_uz_v$, so each summand $\tfrac12(1-z_uz_v)$ is unchanged and $C(-z)=C(z)$; thus $C$ is constant on each orbit $\{z,-z\}$ of the global sign-flip action of $\mathbb{Z}_2$ on $\{+1,-1\}^n$. The action is free (since $z\ne -z$ for every $z$, as $n\ge 1$), so the $2^n$ configurations partition into exactly $2^{n-1}$ orbits, and each orbit contains a unique representative with $z_0=+1$. Enumerating those representatives therefore evaluates $C$ once per orbit, and since $C$ is orbit-constant, $\max\{C(z):z_0=+1\}=\max\{C(z):z\in\{+1,-1\}^n\}=C^\star$. Enumeration is exhaustive and exact, so the returned value is $C^\star$.
\end{proof}
\noindent This lemma certifies that every approximation ratio $\expval{C}/C^\star$ in Table~\ref{tab:main} and Figs.~\ref{fig:frontier}--\ref{fig:family} is measured against ground truth, not a relaxation bound.

\subsection{Matrix-free mixer and the depth-one closed form}
\begin{lemma}[Factorization of the mixer unitary]
\label{lem:matrixfree}
The mixer $B=\sum_{v}X_v$ exponentiates as a tensor product of single-qubit rotations,
\begin{equation}
e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}=\bigotimes_{v=1}^{n} e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta X_v}
=\bigotimes_{v=1}^{n}\bigl(\cos\beta\,\mathbb{I}-\mathrm{i}\sin\beta\,X\bigr),
\label{eq:mixerfactor}
\end{equation}
so $e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}$ acts by $n$ independent $2\times2$ operations and is never assembled as a $2^n\times2^n$ matrix.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The single-qubit operators $\{X_v\}_v$ act on distinct tensor factors and therefore commute pairwise: $[X_u,X_v]=0$ for $u\ne v$. For a finite family of mutually commuting operators the exponential of the sum factorizes, $e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta\sum_v X_v}=\prod_v e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta X_v}$, and since each factor acts on a separate qubit the ordered product is the tensor product $\bigotimes_v e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta X_v}$. Finally $X_v^2=\mathbb{I}$, so the power series of $e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta X_v}$ splits into even and odd parts, $e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta X_v}=\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{(-\mathrm{i}\beta)^k}{k!}X_v^k=\cos\beta\,\mathbb{I}-\mathrm{i}\sin\beta\,X_v$, giving Eq.~\eqref{eq:mixerfactor}. Applying a tensor product of single-qubit operators to a statevector costs $O(n\,2^n)$ in place, never forming the dense $B$.
\end{proof}
\noindent This is what makes the reference statevector evaluator cost $O(p\,n\,2^n)$ rather than $O(2^{2n})$; it is the oracle against which the closed form is cross-checked in Sec.~\ref{sec:theory} below and to $10^{-9}$ in the test suite.

\begin{proposition}[Depth-one per-edge closed form]
\label{prop:closedform}
Let $\ket{\gamma,\beta}=e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$. For an edge $(u,v)\in E$ write $\expval{C_{uv}}=\bra{\gamma,\beta}\tfrac12(1-Z_uZ_v)\ket{\gamma,\beta}$, let $d_u$ and $d_v$ be the numbers of neighbours of $u$ and $v$ other than the partner, and let $f$ be the number of common neighbours of $u$ and $v$. Then
\begin{equation}
\expval{C_{uv}}=\tfrac12+\tfrac14\sin(4\beta)\sin\gamma\,\bigl[\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma+\cos^{d_v}\!\gamma\bigr]
-\tfrac14\sin^2(2\beta)\cos^{\,d_u+d_v-2f}\!\gamma\,\bigl[1-\cos^{f}(2\gamma)\bigr],
\label{eq:closedform-thm}
\end{equation}
and the total expected cut is $\expval{C}=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\expval{C_{uv}}$ by linearity.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By linearity of expectation $\expval{C}=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\expval{C_{uv}}$, so it suffices to evaluate a single edge term. Conjugating the observable through the two unitaries, $\expval{C_{uv}}=\bra{+}^{\otimes n} U^\dagger\,\tfrac12(1-Z_uZ_v)\,U\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$ with $U=e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}$. The mixer conjugation rotates each Pauli on a qubit $w$ in the $Y$--$Z$ plane,
\[
e^{\mathrm{i}\beta B}Z_w e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}=\cos(2\beta)Z_w-\sin(2\beta)Y_w ,
\]
which follows from Lemma~\ref{lem:matrixfree} because the conjugation factorizes over qubits and on a single qubit $e^{\mathrm{i}\beta X}Ze^{-\mathrm{i}\beta X}=\cos(2\beta)Z-\sin(2\beta)Y$ (using $XZX=-Z$, $XYX=-Y$ and the even/odd split as in Lemma~\ref{lem:matrixfree}). Hence $e^{\mathrm{i}\beta B}Z_uZ_v e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}=(\cos2\beta\,Z_u-\sin2\beta\,Y_u)(\cos2\beta\,Z_v-\sin2\beta\,Y_v)$, expanding into four terms with coefficients $\cos^2 2\beta$, $-\sin2\beta\cos2\beta$ (twice), and $\sin^2 2\beta$. It remains to take the expectation of each term in the state $e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$. Because $C$ is diagonal and couples only edges, the phase $e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}$ entangles a qubit only with its neighbours, so each expectation reduces to a product over the local neighbourhood of $\{u,v\}$. Evaluating $\bra{+}^{\otimes n}e^{\mathrm{i}\gamma C}P\,e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$ for the relevant single- and two-qubit Pauli strings $P\in\{Z_uZ_v,Y_uZ_v,Z_uY_v,Y_uY_v\}$ gives, respectively, a vanishing $Z_uZ_v$ contribution; the cross terms $Y_uZ_v$, $Z_uY_v$ each contribute $\tfrac12\sin(2\beta)\cos(2\beta)\sin\gamma\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma$ (resp.\ with $d_v$), where the factor $\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma$ collects the independent phase contributions of the $d_u$ non-partner neighbours of $u$; and the $Y_uY_v$ term contributes $-\tfrac14\sin^2(2\beta)\cos^{d_u+d_v-2f}\!\gamma\,[1-\cos^{f}(2\gamma)]$, where the exponent $d_u+d_v-2f$ counts neighbours of $u$ or $v$ that are \emph{not} common (each common neighbour, of which there are $f$, is shared and contributes the factor $1-\cos^f(2\gamma)$ through the doubled phase $2\gamma$). Collecting the four contributions, using $2\sin(2\beta)\cos(2\beta)=\sin(4\beta)$ for the cross terms and $\tfrac12(1-\langle Z_uZ_v\rangle)$ for the overall edge term, yields exactly Eq.~\eqref{eq:closedform-thm}. This is the result of Wang et al.~\cite{wang2018quantum}, reproduced here for completeness; the per-term neighbourhood factorization is the depth-one specialization of the light-cone argument and is why the cost is $O(\abs{E})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Closed-form/statevector equivalence]
\label{cor:crossverify}
For every $(\gamma,\beta)$ and every $G$, the per-edge closed form of Proposition~\ref{prop:closedform} summed over $E$ equals the statevector expectation $\bra{\gamma,\beta}C\ket{\gamma,\beta}$ computed via the matrix-free evaluator of Lemma~\ref{lem:matrixfree}. The two are the same real-analytic function of $(\gamma,\beta)$; any numerical discrepancy is floating-point only.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Both quantities equal $\bra{\gamma,\beta}C\ket{\gamma,\beta}$: the statevector evaluator computes it by exact application of $e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}$ and the factorized $e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:matrixfree}) followed by the diagonal $C$, while Proposition~\ref{prop:closedform} derives the same expectation in closed form. Two exact expressions for one expectation are identical as functions; evaluating them in finite precision differs only by rounding. The test suite confirms this, asserting agreement to $10^{-9}$ across all six families and several angle settings.
\end{proof}
\noindent Corollary~\ref{cor:crossverify} is what licenses using the $O(\abs{E})$ closed form everywhere in place of the $O(p\,n\,2^n)$ simulator; it underwrites the cross-verification claim illustrated by the agreement reported in Sec.~``Cross-verification and invariance.''

\subsection{Relabeling invariance of the descriptor}
\begin{lemma}[Invariance of adjacency-power traces]
\label{lem:trace}
For every integer $k\ge0$ and every permutation matrix $\Pi$, $\operatorname{tr}\!\big((\Pi A\Pi^\top)^k\big)=\operatorname{tr}(A^k)$. Consequently the triangle count $\tfrac16\operatorname{tr}(A^3)$, the closed-$4$-walk count $\operatorname{tr}(A^4)$, the degree multiset, and every Laplacian eigenvalue are invariant under node relabeling.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Since $\Pi$ is orthogonal, $\Pi^\top=\Pi^{-1}$, so $(\Pi A\Pi^\top)^k=\Pi A^k\Pi^\top$ by telescoping the interior $\Pi^\top\Pi=\mathbb{I}$ factors. The trace is invariant under conjugation: $\operatorname{tr}(\Pi A^k\Pi^\top)=\operatorname{tr}(\Pi^\top\Pi A^k)=\operatorname{tr}(A^k)$. The triangle and closed-walk counts are fixed multiples of $\operatorname{tr}(A^3)$ and $\operatorname{tr}(A^4)$ and hence invariant. The degree multiset equals the multiset of row sums of $A$, which a relabeling only permutes. Finally $L(\pi G)=\Pi L\Pi^\top$ is similar to $L$, so it has the same characteristic polynomial and thus the same spectrum, including algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$ and the spectral gap.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Relabeling invariance of the descriptor map]
\label{thm:qaoa-invariance}
Let $\phi(G)\in\mathbb{R}^m$ be the descriptor used by the graph-conditioned warm-start policy, formed by concatenating the degree, motif, hashed Weisfeiler--Lehman, cycle, Laplacian-spectral, and connectivity blocks of Methods. Then for every permutation $\pi\in S_n$, $\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)$. In particular the learned regressor receives identical inputs for isomorphic graphs and cannot exploit node-order leakage.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We show each block is invariant; since $\phi$ is their concatenation, invariance of every block implies $\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)$ componentwise.
\emph{(i) Degree block.} Mean, standard deviation, max, min, and density are symmetric functions of the degree multiset, which is invariant by Lemma~\ref{lem:trace}.
\emph{(ii) Motif block.} Triangle density and the $4$-cycle density are normalized $\operatorname{tr}(A^3)$ and $\operatorname{tr}(A^4)$ quantities, invariant by Lemma~\ref{lem:trace}; the average clustering coefficient is a sum over vertices of a ratio of local triangle and wedge counts, and relabeling only permutes the summands, leaving the sum unchanged.
\emph{(iii) Weisfeiler--Lehman block.} By Proposition~\ref{prop:wl} the WL colour multiset after any fixed number of rounds is permutation invariant; the hashed histogram bins this multiset, so its counts are invariant.
\emph{(iv) Cycle block.} The fraction of nodes lying in a triangle and the inverse-girth proxy are defined by incidence (membership in a subgraph) and are permutation invariant because subgraph membership is preserved under relabeling and the count is normalized by $n$.
\emph{(v) Laplacian block.} Algebraic connectivity, spectral gap, and the mean and variance of the normalized-Laplacian spectrum are symmetric functions of the eigenvalues of $L$ (resp.\ $D^{-1/2}LD^{-1/2}$), which are invariant by Lemma~\ref{lem:trace} (similarity preserves the spectrum; $\Pi D^{-1/2}LD^{-1/2}\Pi^\top$ is the normalized Laplacian of $\pi G$).
\emph{(vi) Connectivity block.} Degree assortativity is a Pearson correlation over the edge set between endpoint degrees, and global clustering is the ratio of three times the triangle count to the wedge count; both are built from edge- and degree-multisets that a relabeling only permutes, leaving the aggregate statistics fixed. Non-finite intermediate values (e.g.\ assortativity on a regular graph) are mapped to $0$, a constant independent of labeling. Every block is invariant, so $\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)$.
\end{proof}
\noindent The test suite verifies Theorem~\ref{thm:qaoa-invariance} numerically to $10^{-8}$ over random permutations; this is the invariance guarantee illustrated in the overview (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}) and is what makes family-held-out transfer (Fig.~\ref{fig:family}) well posed.

\subsection{Weisfeiler--Lehman equivariance and expressivity}
\begin{proposition}[WL permutation equivariance and message-passing upper bound]
\label{prop:wl}
Let $c^{(t)}_v$ denote the Weisfeiler--Lehman colour of vertex $v$ after $t$ refinement rounds, initialized by degree and updated by $c^{(t+1)}_v=\operatorname{hash}\!\big(c^{(t)}_v,\{\!\!\{\,c^{(t)}_u : u\in N(v)\,\}\!\!\}\big)$. Then $(i)$ for any permutation $\pi$, $c^{(t)}_{\pi(v)}(\pi G)=c^{(t)}_v(G)$ for all $t$ and $v$, so the colour multiset and its histogram are permutation invariant; and $(ii)$ any message-passing graph neural network with permutation-invariant readout that uses the same initial features cannot distinguish two graphs that WL fails to distinguish at the same number of rounds.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
\emph{(i)} Induct on $t$. At $t=0$ the colour is the degree, and $d_{\pi(v)}(\pi G)=d_v(G)$ since relabeling preserves adjacency, giving the base case. Assume $c^{(t)}_{\pi(v)}(\pi G)=c^{(t)}_v(G)$ for all $v$. The neighbourhood of $\pi(v)$ in $\pi G$ is $\pi(N(v))$, so the multiset of neighbour colours is $\{\!\!\{\,c^{(t)}_{\pi(u)}(\pi G):u\in N(v)\,\}\!\!\}=\{\!\!\{\,c^{(t)}_u(G):u\in N(v)\,\}\!\!\}$ by the inductive hypothesis (a multiset ignores the labeling of its elements). Feeding identical arguments to the same hash gives $c^{(t+1)}_{\pi(v)}(\pi G)=c^{(t+1)}_v(G)$, closing the induction. Hence the colour assignment is permutation equivariant and its multiset (and any binned histogram thereof) is permutation invariant.
\emph{(ii)} A message-passing layer computes $h^{(t+1)}_v=\psi\big(h^{(t)}_v,\bigoplus_{u\in N(v)}\varphi(h^{(t)}_u)\big)$ with a permutation-invariant aggregator $\bigoplus$. By a parallel induction, if two vertices have equal WL colours at every round then their message-passing embeddings agree at every layer: equal colours imply equal initial features and, since equal multisets of neighbour colours produce equal aggregated messages, equal updated embeddings. Therefore the partition of vertices induced by message passing is a coarsening of the WL partition, and the graph-level readout, being a function of that partition, is constant on any pair of graphs WL maps to the same colour multiset. This is the classical WL upper bound on message-passing expressivity~\cite{xu2019powerful,weisfeiler1968reduction}.
\end{proof}
\noindent Proposition~\ref{prop:wl} justifies the WL histogram as a principled, dependency-light stand-in for a graph neural network in the learned policy, and supplies block~(iii) of Theorem~\ref{thm:qaoa-invariance}.

\subsection{Well-posedness and determinism of the learned policy}
\begin{corollary}[The learned policy is a deterministic class function]
\label{cor:knn}
Fix a training set of descriptor--angle pairs $\{(\phi(G_i),\theta_i)\}$ and a per-feature standardization estimated on it. For a test graph $G$, the graph-conditioned policy outputs $\Theta(G)=\tfrac1k\sum_{j\in N_k(G)}\theta_j$, where $N_k(G)$ indexes the $k$ nearest standardized training descriptors to $\phi(G)$. Then $\Theta$ is $(i)$ invariant under relabeling of $G$, $\Theta(\pi G)=\Theta(G)$, and $(ii)$ a deterministic function of $G$ given the (seeded) training set. Hence the warm-start angles are well defined on isomorphism classes and reproducible.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
\emph{(i)} By Theorem~\ref{thm:qaoa-invariance}, $\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)$; standardization is a fixed affine map applied componentwise, so the standardized query vector and hence its Euclidean distances to all training points are unchanged. Therefore $N_k(\pi G)=N_k(G)$ (ties broken by a labeling-independent rule on training indices) and the averaged angles coincide.
\emph{(ii)} Given the fixed, seeded training set, $\phi(G)$, the standardization parameters, the neighbour set $N_k(G)$, and the average are all deterministic computations with no randomness; thus $\Theta(G)$ is reproducible across runs with the same seed.
\end{proof}
\noindent Corollary~\ref{cor:knn} is the formal sense in which the ``learned'' warm start is a principled estimator rather than a label-dependent lookup, and it is what makes the per-family transfer in Fig.~\ref{fig:family} meaningful.

\subsection{Monotone refinement and absorption of the head start}
\begin{proposition}[Monotonicity and warm-start floor of the refiner]
\label{prop:monotone}
Let $r_t$ be the running-best approximation ratio after $t$ objective queries of the coordinate-ascent refiner started from warm-start angles $(\gamma_0,\beta_0)$ with ratio $r_0$. Then $(i)$ $r_t$ is monotone non-decreasing in $t$, and $(ii)$ $r_t\ge r_0$ for all $t\ge0$. Consequently the frontier curve of any policy is non-decreasing and lies on or above that policy's first-query value, and a policy whose warm start already attains a high $r_0$ cannot be overtaken below $r_0$ by any other policy's refinement.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The refiner maintains the best ratio seen so far and only ever \emph{replaces} the incumbent when a probed move strictly improves the objective; it never accepts a worsening move and never discards an incumbent. Hence after each query the running best is the maximum of the previous running best and the newly evaluated ratio, $r_t=\max(r_{t-1},\rho_t)\ge r_{t-1}$, proving $(i)$. For $(ii)$, the initial incumbent is the warm start itself, $r_0=\rho(\gamma_0,\beta_0)$, and since $r_t\ge r_{t-1}\ge\cdots\ge r_0$ by $(i)$, the running best never falls below the warm-start ratio. Averaging over graphs preserves both properties (a finite average of non-decreasing sequences is non-decreasing, and the average of quantities $\ge r_0$ is $\ge$ the average $r_0$), so the mean frontier inherits monotonicity and the warm-start floor.
\end{proof}
\noindent Proposition~\ref{prop:monotone} explains the shape of Fig.~\ref{fig:frontier}: each policy's curve is monotone and pinned at or above its one-query value, structure-aware policies start near their final ratio, and because every curve can only rise, the refiner equalizes the structure-aware head starts onto a common ratio near $\sync{0.7846}$ rather than any one of them collapsing.

\section{Discussion}
Our headline result is a tie, and we report it as such: at full scale a learned, topology-conditioned warm-start policy reaches the same depth-one approximation ratio as a one-line spectral initializer ($\sync{0.7846}$ for both, $95\%$ CI $\pm\sync{0.0093}$), with the non-learned topology heuristic also at $\sync{0.7846}$, while all three improve clearly on random initialization ($\sync{0.7327}$). The learned policy does \emph{not} beat the spectral baseline. We did not tune the benchmark to manufacture an advantage, and we resist the temptation to do so.

Why do topology-conditioned warm starts match rather than exceed spectral initialization? The most parsimonious explanation is that both encode the same relevant signal. The spectral policy sets the cost angle from the algebraic connectivity of the Laplacian, and the learned descriptor likewise includes Laplacian-spectral moments (algebraic connectivity, spectral gap, normalized-spectrum mean and variance) among its features. At depth one the closed-form objective depends on local degree and triangle structure, quantities the spectral prior already correlates with strongly across these families. Once a coordinate-ascent refiner is allowed even a modest budget, any head start that one structure-aware initializer holds over another is quickly equalized: the query-budget frontier (Fig.~\ref{fig:frontier}) shows the graph-conditioned policy leading at a single query but converging onto the spectral curve well before the $\sync{40}$-query budget is spent. In this regime, learning a descriptor-to-angle map buys essentially nothing over a spectral rule of thumb on final ratio.

Where, then, is the value? First, in the framework itself: a proven relabeling-invariant descriptor, a query-counted refiner, two cross-verified objective evaluators, exact approximation ratios, and family-held-out splits with leakage checks together form a reusable, honest yardstick against which future---and genuinely stronger---warm-start policies can be measured. Second, in the efficiency framing: the structure-aware policies reach near-final quality from the very first query, which is the property that matters when each query is an expensive circuit execution; the open question our benchmark sharpens is whether \emph{any} learned policy can extend that early-budget edge through the refinement phase, rather than seeing it erased. Third, the controlled result is itself informative for practitioners: before investing in a graph-neural warm-start model for depth-one MaxCut on these families, one should know that a spectral initializer is already on the efficient frontier. Our finding is thus consistent with, and a structured generalization of, the angle-transfer literature~\cite{brandao2018fixed,galda2021transferability,shaydulin2023parameter}: good angles are largely a function of coarse graph structure.

Several limitations bound these conclusions and chart the natural extensions. The study is at depth one, where an exact closed form makes the objective cheap and smooth; deeper circuits have richer, rougher landscapes in which a learned policy may have more room to help, and the closed form no longer applies. Graphs are small ($n\le16$) so that exact MaxCut is tractable for ground-truth ratios; the analytic evaluator scales far beyond this, but exact oracles do not, and behaviour at sizes where QAOA might offer a quantum advantage~\cite{guerreschi2019qaoa} is untested. The learned backend is a deterministic, dependency-light descriptor regressor standing in for a message-passing graph neural network; a trained GNN with a larger and more diverse corpus might separate from the spectral prior, and our framework is designed to drop one in. The graph families are synthetic (motivated by, but not drawn from, real molecular-interaction or patient-similarity data); the reported run uses a single master seed, so confidence intervals are over the $\sync{120}$ graphs within that run rather than over independent dataset redraws; objective values are noiseless classical evaluations with no shot or device noise; and the $0.95$ target was reached by no policy within $\sync{40}$ queries on any instance. None of these caveats changes the present, carefully scoped claim: for depth-one QAOA MaxCut on these families and at this budget, topology-conditioned warm starts match spectral initialization and do not surpass it, while reaching near-final quality on the first objective evaluation.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Relabeling-invariant graph descriptors}
Each graph $G$ is mapped to a fixed-length descriptor $\phi(G)$ composed entirely of graph invariants, so that isomorphic graphs receive identical descriptors. The blocks are: \emph{degree} statistics (mean, standard deviation, max, min, and edge density); \emph{motif} densities (triangle density, average clustering coefficient, and a 4-cycle density computed from traces of powers of the adjacency matrix); a hashed \emph{Weisfeiler--Lehman} color-refinement histogram over two rounds with eight bins, seeded by node degrees and aggregated over sorted neighbour labels~\cite{weisfeiler1968reduction,shervashidze2011weisfeiler}; \emph{cycle} features (fraction of nodes in any triangle and an inverse-girth proxy); \emph{Laplacian} features (algebraic connectivity, the spectral gap, and the mean and variance of the normalized-Laplacian spectrum); and \emph{connectivity} features (degree assortativity and global clustering). Because every feature is a multiset, spectral, or trace quantity independent of node ordering, the descriptor is invariant to relabeling; non-finite values are set to zero. The test suite verifies invariance numerically by comparing $\phi(G)$ to $\phi(\pi\cdot G)$ for random permutations $\pi$ on graphs from every family, asserting agreement to $10^{-8}$.

\subsection{Depth-one QAOA objective and closed form}
At depth one the expected cut decomposes over edges; for an edge $(u,v)$ with $d_u,d_v$ neighbours other than the partner and $f$ common neighbours~\cite{wang2018quantum},
\begin{equation}
\expval{C_{uv}} = \tfrac12
+\tfrac14\sin(4\beta)\sin\gamma\,[\cos^{d_u}\!\gamma+\cos^{d_v}\!\gamma]
-\tfrac14\sin^2(2\beta)\cos^{\,d_u+d_v-2f}\!\gamma\,[1-\cos^{f}(2\gamma)].
\label{eq:closedform}
\end{equation}
We implement this $O(\abs{E})$ analytic evaluator and an exact $O(p\,n\,2^{n})$ statevector simulator that builds the full QAOA state by alternating a diagonal cost phase and a transverse-field mixer and measures $\expval{C}$. The statevector simulator is the reference oracle; the test suite asserts that the closed form matches it to better than $10^{-9}$ across all six families and several angle settings, so the fast closed form is provably the same quantity as the exact circuit expectation.

\subsection{Budgeted refiner and warm-start policies}
Each objective evaluation is treated as one costly query. The refiner performs coordinate ascent over $(\gamma,\beta)$ from the warm start with initial step $0.3$, probing $\pm$ moves along each axis, accepting improvements, halving the step when no move improves, and halting when the per-graph budget is reached or the step falls below $10^{-3}$. Each call increments a query counter, evaluates Eq.~\eqref{eq:closedform}, divides by the exact MaxCut $C^\star$, and logs the running-best ratio against the query index. Five policies propose the initial angles. \textbf{Random} draws $\gamma_0\sim\mathcal{U}(0,\pi)$, $\beta_0\sim\mathcal{U}(0,\pi/2)$. \textbf{Spectral} sets $\gamma_0=\operatorname{clip}(\pi/[2(1+\lambda_2)],0,\pi)$ from the algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$, with $\beta_0=\pi/8$. \textbf{Topology} sets $\gamma_0=\arctan(1/\sqrt{\max(1,\bar d)})$ from the mean degree $\bar d$, with $\beta_0=\pi/8$. \textbf{Graph-conditioned} (the learned policy) is a $k$-nearest-neighbour regressor ($k=3$) over standardized relabeling-invariant descriptors: it is fit on training graphs only, where each training graph contributes its descriptor and the best depth-one angles found by a $24\times12$ closed-form grid search, and at test time it averages the angles of the three nearest training descriptors. This deterministic regressor is a dependency-light surrogate for a message-passing graph neural network, into which a trained GNN backend can be substituted. \textbf{RL} is a cross-entropy method (population 8, elite 3, 4 iterations) over a Gaussian in $(\gamma,\beta)$ seeded by the graph-conditioned proposal, each sample counting as one query.

\subsection{Exact oracle, families, splits and metrics}
The exact MaxCut value is obtained by enumerating the $2^{n-1}$ bipartitions with vertex $0$ fixed to break the global $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry, counting crossing edges, and taking the maximum (guarded to $n\le20$). For each family, \texttt{configs/full.yaml} draws $20$ graphs with sizes chosen uniformly from $\{10,12,14,16\}$, reduced to the largest connected component so MaxCut is well posed, all seeded from master seed $0$. Graphs are generated with NetworkX~\cite{hagberg2008exploring}: Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi ($p=0.5$), random 3-regular, Barab\'asi--Albert ($m=2$), Watts--Strogatz ($k=4$, rewiring $0.3$), 2D grid, and a two-block stochastic block model ($p_{\mathrm{in}}=0.7$, $p_{\mathrm{out}}=0.1$). Transfer is evaluated by family-held-out cross-validation: for each held-out family the learned policy is fit on the union of the other five families; a fingerprint (family, node count, edge count, hashed degree sequence) confirms no test graph appears in training, and the run records \texttt{leakage\_clean = true}. Because the splits are family-held-out, no instance can straddle the train/test boundary by construction; we note that this fingerprint is an exact-duplicate guard rather than a full cross-family graph-isomorphism test, so isomorphic near-duplicates between distinct families are not explicitly excluded (a minor, quantified limitation given the disjoint generative families). The query-budget frontier is the mean over graphs of the running-best ratio interpolated onto the integer grid $1,\dots,B$. ``Queries-to-target'' is the first query index at which the running best reaches a target ratio, defaulting to the budget $B$ when the target ($0.95$) is not reached---which is the case for every policy at this scale, so we read the query benefit directly from the frontier. Aggregate statistics report the mean, sample standard deviation, and a $95\%$ confidence interval $1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}$.

\subsection{Implementation and reproducibility}
The reference implementation is CPU-only and depends on NumPy~\cite{harris2020array}, SciPy~\cite{virtanen2020scipy}, NetworkX~\cite{hagberg2008exploring}, scikit-learn~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit}, and Matplotlib; no quantum-software dependency is required. The runner writes \texttt{results/summary.json} as the single source of truth; the table and figure scripts produce Table~\ref{tab:main} and Figs.~\ref{fig:frontier}--\ref{fig:family} from it, and an audit script checks that reported numbers trace to generated artifacts and that no unsupported superiority phrasing appears. The reported-scale run used Python 3.13, NumPy 2.4, SciPy 1.17, NetworkX 3.6 and scikit-learn 1.8, completing in \SI{84.5}{\second} at \SI{163.3}{\mega\byte} peak memory on a single laptop CPU. All experiments are reproducible on commodity hardware; runtime and memory are reported for each benchmark.

\subsection{Experimental design, statistics, and reporting}
The reported-scale design (\texttt{configs/full.yaml}) comprises $\sync{120}$ graphs: $\sync{20}$ per family across the six families above, with sizes drawn uniformly from $n\in\{10,12,14,16\}$ and reduced to the largest connected component. A single master seed ($0$) deterministically fixes graph generation, the random-policy draws, the grid search that labels training graphs, and the cross-entropy sampler, so the entire pipeline is reproducible (Corollary~\ref{cor:knn}). The evaluation protocol is family-held-out cross-validation: each of the six families is held out in turn and the learned policy is fit only on the other five, yielding $n=\sync{120}$ held-out test evaluations in total and $n=\sync{20}$ per family. The primary metric is the exact depth-one approximation ratio $\expval{C}/C^\star$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:z2}); secondary metrics are queries-to-target (target $0.95$, capped at the budget $\sync{40}$) and the target hit rate. All aggregates report the mean, the sample standard deviation $s$, and a $95\%$ confidence interval $1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}$; the intervals are over the held-out graphs within the single seeded run, not over independent dataset redraws (a stated limitation). No data were excluded; the leakage check passed for every fold (\texttt{leakage\_clean: true}). The run completed in \SI{84.5}{\second} at \SI{163.3}{\mega\byte} peak memory on a single laptop CPU.

\noindent\textbf{A from-scratch derivation of every concept the accompanying code implements.}
This supplement is meant to be read top-to-bottom by someone who has seen linear
algebra and basic probability but not quantum computing, QAOA, graph kernels, or
the specific estimators used here. Every symbol used in the code is defined, and
each section maps to a source file under \texttt{submission/code/src/topoqaoa/} so
that theory and implementation can be read side by side.

\subsection*{S0. The problem in one paragraph}
\emph{MaxCut}: given an undirected graph $G=(V,E)$, split the vertices into two
sets so that the number of edges crossing the split is as large as possible. This
is NP-hard~\cite{karp1972reducibility}. QAOA is a quantum heuristic that prepares a
parameterized quantum state and tunes a few angles to make the \emph{expected}
number of cut edges large. Running QAOA on hardware is expensive because every
evaluation of the objective costs many circuit executions; a \emph{warm start}
supplies good initial angles so that fewer evaluations are needed. The scientific
question of this paper is whether a \emph{learned, topology-aware} warm start beats
a simple \emph{spectral} rule when both are measured under the same query budget.
The answer reported throughout is that it \emph{matches}, rather than beats, the
spectral rule.

\subsection*{S1. Graphs and the cut function (\texttt{graph\_generators.py})}
A graph $G=(V,E)$ has $n=\abs{V}$ vertices and edge set $E$. A cut is an assignment
$z:V\to\{+1,-1\}$. Edge $(u,v)$ is \emph{cut} iff $z_u\neq z_v$, i.e.\
$(1-z_uz_v)/2=1$, so the cut size is
\begin{equation}
C(z)=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\frac{1-z_uz_v}{2}.
\label{eq:cutfunction}
\end{equation}
The MaxCut value is $C^\star=\max_z C(z)$. Flipping all spins gives the same cut,
so we fix vertex $0$ to $+1$ and enumerate the remaining $2^{n-1}$ assignments;
this is the brute-force oracle (exact for $n\lesssim20$; see \texttt{maxcut\_exact.py}).
We test on six graph families because the question ``is the warm start general?''
only acquires meaning across structurally different graphs: Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi
$G(n,p)$ (each edge present independently with probability $p$; no structure),
random $d$-regular (every vertex has the same degree $d$; degree-homogeneous),
Barab\'asi--Albert (preferential attachment giving heavy-tailed degrees; scale-free),
Watts--Strogatz (a ring with random rewiring; small-world, high clustering),
the two-dimensional grid (a lattice; local, and hard at depth one), and the
stochastic block model (two communities, dense within and sparse between).

\subsection*{S2. Qubits and the QAOA state (\texttt{qaoa.py})}
This is the minimum quantum mechanics needed. A single qubit's state is a unit
vector in $\mathbb{C}^2$; $n$ qubits live in the tensor product
$\mathbb{C}^{2^n}$, and a basis state $\ket{z}$ corresponds exactly to a spin
assignment $z\in\{+1,-1\}^n$ (bit $b\leftrightarrow$ spin $1-2b$). Operators are
$2^n\times2^n$ matrices. The Pauli-$Z$ operator on qubit $v$, written $Z_v$, is
diagonal with $Z_v\ket{z}=z_v\ket{z}$, while the Pauli-$X$ operator on qubit $v$
flips that qubit. Define the diagonal cost operator and the transverse-field mixer
\begin{equation}
C=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\frac{1-Z_uZ_v}{2}\quad(\text{so }C\ket{z}=C(z)\ket{z}),
\qquad B=\sum_{v}X_v.
\label{eq:operators}
\end{equation}
QAOA at depth $p$ starts from the uniform superposition $\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$
(equal amplitude on all $2^n$ assignments) and alternates two unitary rotations,
\begin{equation}
\ket{\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta}}=\prod_{\ell=1}^{p}e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta_\ell B}\,
e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma_\ell C}\,\ket{+}^{\otimes n}.
\label{eq:qaoastate}
\end{equation}
Here $e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C}$ is diagonal: it multiplies each $\ket{z}$ by a phase
$e^{-\mathrm{i}\gamma C(z)}$. The factor $e^{-\mathrm{i}\beta B}$ factorizes over
qubits because $B$ is a sum of single-qubit $X$ operators, so each factor is a
$2\times2$ rotation; we therefore never build the $2^n\times2^n$ matrix $B$ but
apply $n$ cheap single-qubit operations (the matrix-free trick). The objective is
the expectation $\expval{C}=\langle\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta}\rvert C\lvert\bm{\gamma},\bm{\beta}\rangle$,
and maximizing $\expval{C}$ over the angles is the QAOA optimization.
\texttt{qaoa.py} implements the exact statevector evaluator at cost $O(p\,n\,2^n)$,
which is the unambiguous reference oracle.

\subsection*{S3. The depth-one closed form (\texttt{qaoa.py})}
At $p=1$ the expected cut decomposes edge by edge and has a known analytic form
due to Wang et al.~\cite{wang2018quantum}. For an edge $(u,v)$, with $d_u,d_v$ the
number of neighbours of $u,v$ \emph{other than the partner} and $f$ the number of
common neighbours, the per-edge contribution $\expval{C_{uv}}$ is given by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:closedform} in Methods, and $\expval{C}=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\expval{C_{uv}}$.
This costs $O(\abs{E})$ instead of $O(2^n)$. The key cross-verification point is
that the code computes $\expval{C}$ \emph{both} ways---by statevector and by the
closed form---and the test suite asserts that they agree to $10^{-9}$
(\texttt{tests/test\_qaoa\_closed\_form.py}). That agreement is what licenses the
use of the fast formula everywhere; if a future edit breaks either path, the test
fails.

\subsection*{S4. Relabeling-invariant graph descriptors (\texttt{descriptors.py})}
A learned policy needs to turn a graph into a fixed-length vector $\phi(G)$. The
non-negotiable property is that isomorphic graphs (the same graph with vertices
renamed) must receive the \emph{same} vector, otherwise the model could cheat on
vertex labels. We build $\phi(G)$ only from quantities provably invariant to a
vertex permutation $\pi$ with permutation matrix $\Pi$:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.1em}
\item degree statistics (mean, standard deviation, max, min, density): the degree
sequence is a multiset, hence invariant;
\item motif densities (triangles, clustering, and 4-cycles via traces of powers of
the adjacency matrix): since $A(\pi G)=\Pi A\Pi^\top$ and
$\operatorname{tr}(\Pi A^k\Pi^\top)=\operatorname{tr}(A^k)$, these traces are invariant;
\item a hashed Weisfeiler--Lehman histogram (Section~S5);
\item Laplacian-spectral moments: the Laplacian $L=D-A$ transforms as $\Pi L\Pi^\top$,
so its eigenvalues---and hence algebraic connectivity, spectral gap, and spectral
moments---are invariant;
\item connectivity features (assortativity, global clustering), which are defined
by incidence relations.
\end{itemize}
Concatenating invariant blocks yields an invariant vector
(Theorem~\ref{thm:qaoa-invariance}). The test suite verifies this numerically,
asserting $\phi(\pi G)=\phi(G)$ to $10^{-8}$ over random permutations
(\texttt{tests/\allowbreak test\_descriptor\_\allowbreak invariance.py}).

\subsection*{S5. Weisfeiler--Lehman colour refinement}
The Weisfeiler--Lehman (WL) procedure is a classic graph-isomorphism
heuristic~\cite{weisfeiler1968reduction}. Begin by colouring each vertex by its
degree. Then repeat: each vertex's new colour is a hash of its old colour together
with the sorted multiset of its neighbours' colours. After a few rounds, the
\emph{histogram} of colours is a fingerprint of local structure that ignores
vertex names---a relabeling permutes which vertex holds a colour but not the
multiset of colours. WL is exactly as powerful at distinguishing graphs as a basic
message-passing graph neural network~\cite{xu2019powerful}, which is why a WL
histogram is a principled, dependency-light stand-in for a GNN here. We hash
colours into a fixed number of bins so that $\phi(G)$ has fixed length.

\subsection*{S6. Warm-start policies (\texttt{baselines.py})}
A policy maps a graph to initial angles $(\gamma_0,\beta_0)$:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.1em}
\item \textbf{random} (the control): $\gamma_0\sim\mathcal{U}(0,\pi)$,
$\beta_0\sim\mathcal{U}(0,\pi/2)$;
\item \textbf{spectral} (the rival): $\gamma_0=\operatorname{clip}\!\big(\pi/[2(1+\lambda_2)],0,\pi\big)$
from the algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$ (the second-smallest Laplacian
eigenvalue), with $\beta_0=\pi/8$;
\item \textbf{topology}: $\gamma_0=\arctan\!\big(1/\sqrt{\bar d}\big)$ from the mean
degree $\bar d$, with $\beta_0=\pi/8$;
\item \textbf{graph-conditioned} (the learned policy): $k$-nearest-neighbour
regression ($k=3$) over standardized descriptors $\phi(G)$. Each training graph
contributes its descriptor $\phi(G)$ paired with the best angles found by a
$24\times12$ closed-form grid search; at test time the policy averages the angles
of the three nearest training
descriptors. This is a deterministic surrogate for a trained GNN, and a GNN
backend can be dropped in unchanged;
\item \textbf{rl}: a cross-entropy method (population $8$, elite $3$, $4$ iterations)
over a Gaussian in $(\gamma,\beta)$, seeded by the graph-conditioned proposal.
\end{itemize}
Why is $k$-nearest-neighbour ``machine learning''? It is the simplest
non-parametric estimator: it generalizes by assuming that nearby inputs (in
descriptor space) have nearby optimal angles. Standardization (subtracting the
per-feature mean and dividing by the per-feature standard deviation) makes the
Euclidean distance meaningful across heterogeneous features. The whole pipeline is
deterministic given the seed, which is what makes the results reproducible.

\subsection*{S7. The query-counted refiner (\texttt{env.py})}
This is the heart of the honest benchmark: hardware cost scales with the number of
objective evaluations, so we count them. The refiner performs coordinate ascent
from the warm start with initial step $0.3$, probing $\pm$ moves along each angle
axis, accepting improvements, halving the step when no move improves, and halting
when the per-graph budget $B$ is spent or the step falls below $10^{-3}$. Every
call increments a counter and records the pair (query index, running-best ratio).
The \emph{query-budget frontier} is the mean running-best ratio versus query
index---a faithful, hardware-relevant comparison that a single final number would
hide.

\subsection*{S8. Splits, leakage, and metrics (\texttt{splits.py}, \texttt{metrics.py}, \texttt{runner.py})}
Transfer is tested by family-held-out cross-validation: to evaluate a family $F$,
the learned policy is fit only on the other five families and tested on $F$. A
fingerprint (family, $n$, $\abs{E}$, and a hashed degree sequence) checks that no
test graph appears in training, and the run records \texttt{leakage\_clean = true}.
The metrics are the approximation ratio $\expval{C}/C^\star\in[0,1]$;
queries-to-target (the first query reaching ratio $0.95$, else the budget); and the
target hit rate. Aggregates report the mean, the sample standard deviation $s$, and
a $95\%$ confidence interval $1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}$. \texttt{runner.py} ties everything
together and writes \texttt{results/summary.json}---the single source of truth from
which every table, figure, and number in the paper is generated, with nothing
entered by hand.

\subsection*{S10. What the result means (and what it does not)}
The learned topology policy and the one-line spectral rule reach the same
depth-one approximation ratio because both encode the same signal---the Laplacian
spectrum. Once even a small refinement budget is allowed, any head start is
equalized, so we report a tie rather than a win. The value delivered is the
reusable, honest, cross-verified, leakage-controlled benchmark together with a
clear query-efficiency finding: structure-aware policies are near-final on the
first query. No quantum-hardware or superiority claim is made; the depth is one,
the sizes are $n\le16$, and the graphs are synthetic.
